### Take time to sharpen your axe. 
1. Reserve some time for sharpening your axe. (Preferably the earlier the better)    
2. Your tools should be well maintained
    - In an industrial pipeline, there are dedicated teams for maintaining the tools. In research, you are responsible for maintaining your own tools.
    - This includes your laptop, software, and also your own mental and physical health.
    - In scrum methodology, a seperate time frame (~10-15%) is dedicated to code refactoring. Similarly, you should have a dedicated time for cleanup. (Eg code refactrings, cleaning up your desk, cleaning up your digital workspace, organizing the folders, etc)

### Don't rush through things. 
1. Going slow and concrete is better than rushing up and then doing re-work. 
2. There is no pressure of time. Pressure makes bad decisions. Good research cannot happen under time pressure. 
3. It's time to relax and rewind. 
4. Don't be afraid to take breaks. Taking breaks is important for your mental health and also for your productivity. It will help you to come up with new ideas and also to see things from a different perspective.
5. Take time to revise and look over what all you have done. Sometimes it may be that you will find new ideas or find mistakes when you go through your work. A nice way of going through your work is explaining it to someone else. You can explain it to your guide, or to your friends, or even to yourself. This will help you to understand your work better and also to find new ideas. Take a step back and look at the bigger picture. This will help you to go in the right direction rather than rushing blindly through in an unknown direction. 


### Plan for disruptions

Sometimes there will be situations that will disrupt your normal flow of work. In. such cases, you must prepare early on so that your time is not wasted. You should have some work to do, saved, when 
1. Internet goes off (Keep some work that does not require internet access, saved on your laptop)
2. Electricity goes off (Printed material, or work that can be done on a paper)
3. You are travelling (Some thinking work / Audiobooks / Podcasts)
4. Your laptop goes for repairing
5. You are attending any conference/function etc where you want to work (no laptop access) (Some thinking work, some printed books/papers to read)

Always a good idea to carry a small notebook and pen with you. You can jot down ideas whenever they come to your mind.


### Weekly reviews
1. Set agenda two days before the meeting. 
2. It is not important to showcase that you have done a lot. But it is important to have detailed discussions on the small things that you have done. 
3. As far as possible, record every meeting (either audio or video or transcript) for online meetings. 
4. After every meeting, write a small summary of the meeting and mail it in a separate thread. Make sure to note down the action items and deadlines in that mail. This will help you to keep track of your work and also to keep your guide updated about your work. It will also help you to prepare for the next meeting.  Note down:
    1. What was discussed in the meeting
    2. What are the action items
    3. Who is responsible for each action item
    4. What are the deadlines for each action item  
    5. Actionables for the next week
    6. Suggestions 
    7. Feedback (if any)
5. Maintain daily, weekly worklog. Note that this worklog is for your own reference and not to be shared with anyone else. You may share parts of the worklog (or monthly, weekly summaries of the worklog) with your guide.
